Competition Overview: CanSat is a design‚Äìbuild‚Äìfly challenge where a soda‚Äëcan‚Äësized payload is taken through a full aerospace process, run by the American Astronautical Society with support from the U.S. Naval Research Laboratory and NASA. Teams work like a small flight program: PDR (Preliminary Design Review) ‚Üí CDR (Critical Design Review) ‚Üí environmental tests (drop/thermal/vibration as applicable) ‚Üí FRR (Flight Readiness Review) ‚Üí launch ‚Üí post‚Äëflight data review.

2024 Mission Profile: Our 2024 CanSat mission simulated a space probe entering a planetary atmosphere, tasked with safeguarding a delicate instrument‚Äîrepresented by a raw egg‚Äîthroughout its flight. The mission began during the rocket's ascent, where the CanSat, acting as the nose cone, measured ascent speed until its violent deployment at 725 meters. Following deployment, it entered the descent phase with a heat shield, deploying an aero-braking shield to maintain a stable orientation and a controlled descent rate of 10-30 m/s, verified by an onboard tilt sensor. At an altitude of 100 meters, the mission executed a critical transition: the heat shield detached and a parachute deployed, sharply reducing the descent speed to under 5 m/s to ensure a safe landing with the egg intact. Throughout all phases, the CanSat continuously transmitted telemetry data at a 1 Hz rate via a bidirectional communication link, relaying readings from sensors tracking altitude, temperature, battery voltage, GPS position, and stability (measured by a pitot tube and camera) to the ground station, which could also send necessary commands back to the probe.

Team: Our team consisted of 11 multidisciplinary students from the Departments of Electrical and Electronic Engineering (EEE) and Computer Science and Engineering (CSE), whose diverse skills were instrumental in the project‚Äôs success. Contributions ranged from developing a web-based interface for real-time data visualization and sensor integration with custom PCB design, to creating control algorithms, communication and data handling, and performing CAD modeling and 3D printing for the physical architecture of the CanSat. The project was generously supported financially by United International University (UIU) and United Group.

My Contribution:

1. Autonomous Control (Flight Software): I designed and implemented the autonomous control system using a robust Finite State Machine (FSM) in C++, governing six critical mission states from Pre-Launch to Landed. The system relied on multi-sensor fusion‚Äîsynthesizing data from barometric pressure (for altitude), acceleration data from IMU sensor (for z axis position calculation), tilt sensor, an ultrasonic sensor, and a pitot tube (for velocity)‚Äîto make accurate decisions. To ensure fault tolerance, the logic required 20 consecutive confirming readings before any state transition, preventing false triggers. This architecture autonomously managed the entire flight profile: detecting ascent via positive altitude difference and accelerometers values, identifying apogee to initiate descent, triggering heat shield release at 110 meters, deploying the parachute after a precise 350ms delay, and confirming landing upon detecting altitude below 5 meters and velocity under 1 m/s to activate a final buzzer signal, guaranteeing full mission autonomy and payload safety.

2. Physical Structure: I was responsible for conceptualizing the CanSat's overall structure to meet stringent aerodynamic and weight constraints. I developed a physics-based design methodology for the systems, optimizing the aerobraking heat shield's geometry to achieve a stable descent velocity within the target range of 10‚Äì30 m/s and engineering the parachute deployment system to ensure a safe landing under 5 m/s. I coordinated closely with a dedicated design team to transform these functional requirements into detailed, actionable CAD models. The resulting heat shield used a spring-loaded, pentagonal design with ripstop nylon, and its deployment was triggered by a servo-actuated push-lock release mechanism. Similarly, the parachute system was housed in a custom chamber where, upon command, a servo released a spring-hinged door, and two linear springs ejected the parachute for clean deployment. I directed the material selection strategy, choosing ABS for its optimal strength-to-weight ratio, and oversaw the additive manufacturing process‚Äîtroubleshooting challenges such as warping and layer adhesion through controlled printing and post-processing with epoxy‚Äîto ensure all components were robust, lightweight, and mission-ready.

Competition Overview: University Rover Challenge (URC) is an international field robotics competition run by The Mars Society in the Mars Desert Research Station, Utah. URC challenges student teams to design and build the next generation of Mars rovers that will one day work alongside astronauts exploring the Red Planet. Teams are scored across mission tasks (Science, Autonomy, Equipment Servicing, Extreme Retrieval) and documentation/presentations, operating under time, terrain, and communications constraints.

2022 Competition Challenge: URC challenges students to build remotely operated rovers that can accomplish a variety of tasks. Rovers will compete in four missions: 1) a Science Mission to investigate a site for the presence of life; 2) a Delivery Mission to deliver a variety of objects to astronauts in the field across rugged terrain; 3) an Equipment Servicing Mission to perform dexterous operations on a mock lander using a robotic arm; and 4) an Autonomous Navigation Mission to autonomously travel to a series of locations and find objects. A drone (aerial vehicle) may be used to assist the rover in the Delivery Mission, but is not required.

Team: The UIU Mars Rover team consisted of 17 multidisciplinary members from the Electrical and Electronic Engineering (EEE) and Computer Science and Engineering (CSE) departments. To efficiently tackle the complex requirements of the competition, the team was organized into six specialized sub-teams: the Mechanical and Electrical teams collaboratively developed the rover‚Äôs chassis, wheels, robotic arm, and control systems for the Equipment Servicing and Delivery missions; the Software team engineered the autonomous navigation system and developed operational web software; the Communication team designed the robust wireless communication system to maintain a critical data link with the ground station; the Science team designed a life-detection protocol and scientific exploration subsystem for in-situ experiments; and the Logistics and Branding team coordinated project management and public outreach.

We also developed a pipeline of multiple binary classifiers trained on a custom dataset of 765 rock samples involving deep neural networks and transfer learning to effectively classify rock samples without any physical contact. Autonomous Wildlife intrusion detection & response system Ongoing

  • Developing a ROS-based network integrating IP cameras and autonomous ground robots for real-time animal intrusion detection and mitigation in monitored areas.
  • System triggers alert, deploys a SLAM-enabled mobile robot for localization, path planning, and live video streaming, using computer vision to track and distract the animal until field teams arrive, optimizing robot usage compared to traditional UAV/UGV surveillance methods.

This project focuses on a significant challenge in human-wildlife coexistence: the real-time detection and mitigation of animal intrusions in residential or monitored areas. Initiated in collaboration with a partner on August 20, 2025, our goal is to develop a fully autonomous, ROS-based monitoring and response system.

The system's architecture integrates an IP camera network for continuous surveillance with an autonomous mobile ground robot for on-site intervention. The initial detection phase utilizes computer vision algorithms on the camera feeds to identify and classify it as an animal not human. Upon detection, the system transmits an immediate alert to a designated response team and concurrently triggers the deployment of an autonomous mobile robot.

The robot's navigation is a key technical aspect, drawing inspiration from our Mars rover navigation systems. It receives the animal's GPS coordinates as its initial target and then utilizes Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM) for real-time localization and dynamic mapping of the environment. This SLAM capability, paired with a local planner, allows the robot to navigate through dynamic obstacles with precision as it approaches its target. Once in the vicinity, the robot employs its own computer vision system to precisely detect the animal's species and track its location, maintaining a safe, pre-defined distance. Its primary function is to employ non-harmful distraction techniques (e.g., emitting specific audible frequencies, flashing lights) to deter the animal until the human response team arrives. During this entire process, the robot streams live video footage to a local server, providing the rescue team with up-to-the-minute situational awareness and critical visual data to aid their response.

Our current development phase is centered on a meticulous study of the core architectural principles and a thorough evaluation of the necessary algorithms. We are actively researching SLAM methodologies, path-planning stacks like NAV2, and modern computer vision models such as YOLO (You Only Look Once) to inform our implementation strategy. This foundational research is a crucial step for building and validating the system's core functionalities through high-fidelity simulations in Gazebo and RViz. This disciplined, phased approach ensures we develop a robust and viable system architecture. The immediate goal of this phase is to produce a compelling simulation to secure funding for the fabrication and real-world deployment of a physical prototype.

sEMG Signal-based Prosthetic Hand Control using Embedded ML (Tiny ML) with Deep Neural Network Capstone Project

Controlled a robotic hand using EMG signals with a novel forearm electrode placement strategy and real time signal processing for four gesture classification. Deployed a TinyML-based DNN model on an embedded device, achieving 87.5% accuracy with responsive performance. [C++]

Read moreRead less ‚ñ∂

This project was conducted in collaboration with a startup company aiming to pioneer research in affordable biomedical robotics. Our goal was to create a functional and cost-effective Ai-driven control system for a prosthetic hand using surface Electromyography (sEMG) signals, overcoming the cost barriers of traditional high-compute solutions.

The system's development followed a meticulous, research-driven pipeline:

1. Novel Sensor Placement Strategy: The project began with extensive research into the anatomical and physiological structure of the human forearm, studying muscle groups and tendon movement to determine optimal signal acquisition points. This foundational research led to the development of a novel, multi-band electrode strategy, with sensors placed strategically on the shoulder, upper arm, and forearm. This approach captured a comprehensive set of muscle activation patterns, providing richer data for accurate gesture classification compared to traditional single-location methods.

2. Signal Acquisition & Advanced Pre-processing: Raw sEMG signals from the sensors were processed through a custom signal conditioning algorithm. This step was critical for filtering noise, amplifying relevant features, and preparing clean data for the machine learning model, ensuring signal integrity and reliability.

3. Deep Learning Model Development: We then utilized a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to train a deep learning model. This model learns the complex patterns in the sEMG data to accurately classify four distinct hand gestures, enabling intuitive prosthetic control.

4. TinyML Deployment for Real-Time Inference: To achieve a truly low-cost and portable system, we converted the trained CNN into a smaller, optimized Deep Neural Network (DNN) and deployed it onto a low-power microcontroller using Edge Impulse (built on TensorFlow Lite)‚Äî a process known as TinyML. This eliminated the need for a continuous connection to a desktop computer or a high-cost embedded platform.

5. System Integration & Performance: The entire inference pipeline runs in real-time on the microcontroller. The system analyzes incoming sEMG signals and triggers the corresponding prosthetic hand movement with minimal latency, achieving a final classification accuracy of 87.5%.

Impact & Achievement: This work successfully demonstrated a significant reduction in system cost and increase in portability without compromising on performance. The project's novelty and success were recognized through multiple awards and its publication in a major IEEE conference, validating its contribution to the field of accessible biomedical robotics.
