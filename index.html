Competition Overview: CanSat is a design–build–launch competition in space robotics domain where a soda‑can‑sized payload is taken through a full aerospace process, run by the American Astronautical Society with support from the U.S. Naval Research Laboratory and NASA. Teams work like a small flight program: PDR (Preliminary Design Review) → CDR (Critical Design Review) → environmental tests (drop/thermal/vibration as applicable) → FRR (Flight Readiness Review) → launch → post‑flight data review.

2024 Mission Profile: Our 2024 CanSat mission simulated a space probe entering a planetary atmosphere, tasked with safeguarding a delicate instrument—represented by a raw egg—throughout its flight. The mission began during the rocket's ascent, where the CanSat, acting as the nose cone, measured ascent speed until its violent deployment at 725 meters. Following deployment, it entered the descent phase with a heat shield, deploying an aero-braking shield to maintain a stable orientation and a controlled descent rate of 10-30 m/s, verified by an onboard tilt sensor. At an altitude of 100 meters, the mission executed a critical transition: the heat shield detached and a parachute deployed, sharply reducing the descent speed to under 5 m/s to ensure a safe landing with the egg intact. Throughout all phases, the CanSat continuously transmitted telemetry data at a 1 Hz rate via a bidirectional communication link, relaying readings from sensors tracking altitude, temperature, battery voltage, GPS position, and stability (measured by a pitot tube and camera) to the ground station, which could also send necessary commands back to the probe.

Team: Our team consisted of 11 multidisciplinary students from the Departments of Electrical and Electronic Engineering (EEE) and Computer Science and Engineering (CSE), whose diverse skills were instrumental in the project’s success. Contributions ranged from developing a web-based interface for real-time data visualization and sensor integration with custom PCB design, to creating control algorithms, communication and data handling, and performing CAD modeling and 3D printing for the physical architecture of the CanSat. The project was generously supported financially by United International University (UIU) and United Group.

My Contribution:

1. Autonomous Control Operation (Flight Software): I was responsible for the autonomous control system, which I designed and implemented using a robust Finite State Machine (FSM) in C++. This system governed the six critical mission phases, from Pre-Launch to Landed. To make accurate decisions, the FSM relied on multi-sensor fusion. It continuously synthesized data from a barometric pressure sensor (for altitude), an IMU (for acceleration and Z-axis position), a tilt sensor, an ultrasonic sensor, and a pitot tube (for velocity). A key feature of my design was its fault tolerance. The logic was programmed to require 20 consecutive confirming readings before executing any state transition, effectively preventing false triggers from sensor noise. This architecture successfully managed the entire flight profile autonomously. It detected ascent, identified apogee to begin descent, triggered the heat shield release at 110 meters, and deployed the parachute after a precise 350ms delay. Finally, it confirmed landing by checking for an altitude below 5 meters and a velocity under 1 m/s, activating a final buzzer signal to ensure full mission autonomy and payload safety. [ Referrence→ Technical Document: UIU CanSat, see Section(8), Page(13-14)]

2. Physical Structure: My primary responsibility was conceptualizing the CanSat's overall structure to meet stringent aerodynamic and weight constraints. I developed a physics-based approach to design two critical systems: the aerobraking heat shield and the parachute deployment system. My goal was to optimize the heat shield's geometry for a stable descent velocity between 10–30 m/s, and to engineer the parachute system to guarantee a safe landing under 5 m/s. I worked closely with the design team to translate these functional requirements into detailed, actionable CAD models. The resulting design featured a spring-loaded, pentagonal heat shield made from ripstop nylon. Its deployment was triggered by a servo-actuated push-lock release mechanism. Similarly, for the parachute, I designed a custom chamber where, upon command, a servo would release a spring-hinged door, and two linear springs would eject the parachute for a clean deployment. A key part of my role was directing the material selection and fabrication. I chose ABS for its optimal strength-to-weight ratio and personally managed the 3D printing process. This involved troubleshooting common challenges like warping and poor layer adhesion by refining print settings and using adhesives for post-processing. My hands-on oversight ensured every component was robust, lightweight, and mission-ready. [ Referrence→ Technical Document: UIU CanSat, see Section(5.5,5.6,6.2), Page(7-10)]

3. Team Leadership: As a team lead, I took the lead on project management and documentation, in addition to my technical contributions. I authored comprehensive documents, including preliminary design, critical design, environmental test, and flight readiness reviews, to ensure rigorous validation and competition compliance. Furthermore, I represented our team as the Mission Control Officer during the on-field competition in Virginia, USA, where I effectively communicated & presented our design philosophy and technical execution to judges, justified engineering choices during rigorous design reviews, and demonstrated our system's capability, which played a key role in securing our final standing

Competition Overview: University Rover Challenge (URC) is an international field robotics competition run by The Mars Society in the Mars Desert Research Station, Utah. URC challenges student teams to design and build the next generation of Mars rovers that will one day work alongside astronauts exploring the Red Planet. Teams are scored across mission tasks (Science, Autonomy, Equipment Servicing, Extreme Retrieval) and documentation/presentations, operating under time, terrain, and communications constraints.

2022 Competition Challenge: URC challenges students to build remotely operated rovers that can accomplish a variety of tasks. Rovers will compete in four missions: 1) a Science Mission to investigate a site for the presence of life; 2) a Delivery Mission to deliver a variety of objects to astronauts in the field across rugged terrain; 3) an Equipment Servicing Mission to perform dexterous operations on a mock lander using a robotic arm; and 4) an Autonomous Navigation Mission to autonomously travel to a series of locations and find objects. A drone (aerial vehicle) may be used to assist the rover in the Delivery Mission, but is not required.

Team: The UIU Mars Rover team consisted of 17 multidisciplinary members from the Electrical and Electronic Engineering (EEE) and Computer Science and Engineering (CSE) departments. To efficiently tackle the complex requirements of the competition, the team was organized into six specialized sub-teams: the Mechanical and Electrical teams collaboratively developed the rover’s chassis, wheels, robotic arm, and control systems for the Equipment Servicing and Delivery missions; the Software team engineered the autonomous navigation system and developed operational web software; the Communication team designed the robust wireless communication system to maintain a critical data link with the ground station; the Science team designed a life-detection protocol and scientific exploration subsystem for in-situ experiments; and the Logistics and Branding team coordinated project management and public outreach.

My Contribution:

1. Science Sub-System: We developed a scientific exploration subsystem involving a three DoF secondary manipulator and analysis chamber to categorize any soil and rock sample into extant, extinct or ‘No Presence of Life’. Mesh of multiple end effectors and a circular scoop motion can effectively collect up to four soil samples preventing any cross-contamination alongside the UV lights in the funnel. The samples are each deposited into 3 isolated beakers for the detection of biomolecules with chemical tests, which involve: Protein using ninhydrin solution, Lipid using Sudan III solution and Carbohydrate using benedict's solution. Qualitative color changes are observed through a camera and the results are then streamed back to the base station for immediate evaluation and feedback as input to our customized algorithm, classifying the sample into Extant, Extinct, and/or No Presence of Life. USB microscope feedback along with the presence of mVOC sensors have been used to detect whether a rock sample is Extant. We also developed a pipeline of multiple binary classifiers trained on a custom dataset of 765 rock samples involving deep neural networks and transfer learning to effectively classify rock samples without any physical contact. The outcomes of the sensor feedback and model predictions have been used to classify a rock sample. The entire rover and its scientific operations were remotely controlled via a robust wireless communication system. [ Referrence→ Science Document, see Section(1,2), Page(2-4)]

Autonomous wildlife monitoring & deterrence system Ongoing

Currently developing a ROS-based system where a camera network detects wildlife and autonomously deploys a rover for non-harmful deterrence. The rover utilizes SLAM and path planning for navigation and provides live video streaming to assist human response teams.

Read moreRead less ▶

This project focuses on a significant challenge in human-wildlife coexistence: the real-time detection and mitigation of animal intrusions in residential or monitored areas. Initiated in collaboration with Abdullah Al masud, Research Engineer of Green University of Bangladesh on September 20, 2025, our goal is to develop a fully autonomous, ROS-based monitoring and deterence system.

Planned System Architecture: The designed system will be built on a two-part architecture. First, a fixed camera network will use computer vision algorithms to continuously scan for animals. Once a detection is confirmed and classified as non-human, the system will calculate it’s GPS Coordinate and send an immediate alert to a response team, including the animal’s location. Simultaneously, the system will deploy an autonomous rover. The rover will receive the animal's GPS coordinates from the camera network and navigate to the area using SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping) for real-time positioning and dynamic obstacle avoidance. Upon arrival, the rover's own vision system will take over to identify the species and track the animal's movements while maintaining a safe distance. Its primary role will be non-harmful deterrence, using methods like specific audible frequencies or flashing lights. Throughout the entire process, the rover will stream live video back to a local server, giving the human team full situational awareness before they arrive.

Current Status & Development Strategy: We are currently in the foundational research and simulation phase. Our focus is on the core algorithms, including SLAM methodologies, the NAV2 path-planning stack, and modern computer vision models like YOLO. We are building and validating the system's architecture through simulations in Gazebo and RViz. This disciplined approach is designed to produce a compelling simulation to secure funding for this project.

sEMG Signal-based Prosthetic Hand Control using Embedded ML (Tiny ML) with Deep Neural Network Capstone Project

Controlled a robotic hand using EMG signals via a novel forearm electrode placement strategy and a real-time signal processing pipeline for four-gesture classification. A custom DNN was trained and deployed as a TinyML model on a low-cost embedded device, achieving 87.5% accuracy with responsive performance.

Read moreRead less ▶

This project was conducted in collaboration with a startup company aiming to pioneer research in affordable biomedical robotics. Our goal was to create a functional and cost-effective Ai-driven control system for a prosthetic hand using surface Electromyography (sEMG) signals, overcoming the cost barriers of traditional high-compute solutions.

The system's development followed a meticulous, research-driven pipeline:

1. Novel Sensor Placement Strategy: The project began with extensive research into the anatomical and physiological structure of the human forearm, studying muscle groups and tendon movement to determine optimal signal acquisition points. This foundational research led to the development of a novel, multi-band electrode strategy, with sensors placed strategically on the shoulder, upper arm, and forearm. This approach captured a comprehensive set of muscle activation patterns, providing richer data for accurate gesture classification compared to traditional single-location methods.

2. Signal Acquisition & Advanced Pre-processing: Raw sEMG signals from the sensors were processed through a custom signal processing algorithm. This step was critical for filtering noise, amplifying relevant features, and preparing clean data for the machine learning model, ensuring signal integrity and reliability.

3. Deep Learning Model Development: I designed and trained a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) with the processed signals to learn the complex patterns in the sEMG signals for accurately classifying four distinct hand gestures.

4. TinyML Deployment for Real-Time Inference: To achieve a truly low-cost and portable system, I converted the trained CNN into a smaller, optimized Deep Neural Network (DNN) and deployed it onto a low-power microcontroller using Edge Impulse (built on TensorFlow Lite)— a process known as TinyML. This eliminated the need for a continuous connection to a desktop computer or a high-cost embedded platform.

5. System Integration & Performance: The entire inference pipeline runs in real-time on the microcontroller. The system analyzes incoming sEMG signals and triggers the corresponding prosthetic hand movement with minimal latency, achieving a final classification accuracy of 87.5%.

Impact & Achievement: This work successfully demonstrated a significant reduction in system cost and increase in portability without compromising on performance. The project's novelty and success were recognized through multiple awards and its publication in a major IEEE conference, validating its contribution to the field of accessible biomedical robotics.
